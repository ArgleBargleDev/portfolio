Organic Enemy AI Prototype

Combat Showcase

This is an old prototype I made in Adobe Flash, back when that was a thing. The graphics are a bit unfinished and Flash's virtual machine really bottlenecked performance, but the AI I wrote still holds up pretty well!

All enemies in this video are piloted by the AI, including the mirror match players, aside from that one part where I pilot one of the skeleton enemies for fun.

Reacting to the Frame Data

At the core of this AI is its ability to read the frame data for the animations of the character it's piloting. Whenever the AI would make a decision, it analyzes each of the moves it can use, and compares the timing and placements of hitboxes and hurtboxes in relation to the player it's attacking. If the player is currently performing a move, it reads their frame data as well. And when the player isn't doing anything in particular, the AI will try and predict what the player is capable of if they were to trigger a move at that moment (this is why enemies will sometimes preemptively block when they see you jumping at them, before you even press the attack button).

With this, and without any special case coding, the enemy has innate knowledge of when to put up their shield, how to use shield-breaker moves, when to use gap closers or dodge moves, how to capitalize on their opponent's long recovery time, how to jump over attacks, even how to take one step backward so an attack's hitbox will miss!

Since the AI is reading the frame data and making these decisions on the fly, there isn't actually any unique AI code for any of the different enemies. Even weird enemies (like the little orbs that only have one attack they can do while jumping) know how to pilot themselves as soon as the animations are done. This made the development of new enemies fast and easy!

Introducing Imperfections

Obviously an AI that can read the animations is going to be frame-accurate in their defense and attack. When this system was first implemented, the AI would dodge every single one of my attacks and beat me relentlessly.

As payback, I decided to give it a simulation of human error. I gave the AI a reaction time variable, which makes all the frame data is can see slightly outdated, allowing it to misread the situation. To add realism, I made this value fluctuate depending on what the opponent was doing, what the AI itself is doing, how many attacks the opponent is doing compared to the AI, how many other enemies are nearby, etc. This fluctuates throughout the fight, allowing you to break the enemy's focus if you rush them down. Likewise, if you are being too predictable, the enemy will become more vigilant.

If the enemy is sufficiently stressed out, they can hallucinate their opponent doing the wrong moves. This causes their reactions to become erratic, but still realistic, since they're reacting to what could be happening, and jumping to incorrect conclusions just like a human does.

Confidence and Cowardice

Finally, to give the enemies a bit more personality, I added a confidence variable. High confidence makes the AI over-value the success of their moves, where low confidence makes them over-value the success of their opponent's moves. This results in low confidence enemies keeping their distance and playing very defensive, while high confidence enemies go crazy and launch themselves to wherever you are, ignoring the imminent danger you pose.

Confidence also fluctuates throughout the fight. If the enemy is suffering a lot of hit-stun, or if their opponent is being very active, their confidence will decrease. However, their confidence will increase if they're maintaining an advantageous position in combat, or if their opponent is simply not blocking very much.

All of these factors result in an enemy AI that plays these characters much like a normal human would, resulting in very dynamic fights for enemies of all types!
